History of Climbing

Climbing was fairly obsolete as a recreational activity for many years. The original climbing started in an occupational manner, often as tree fallers and other necessary jobs during the late 19th century, and into the early 1900s. In the 1930s, several "dirt-bag" climbers began to modernize the once "necessity-only" sport into a more recreational way of living.

The well known activity of big wall climbing began in the 1950s with early climbers such as Royal Robbins, Tom Frost, and Walter Bonatti. During the formative years of Rock Climbing, the gear was often very rugged. Going into the more modern years of climbing such as the 70s, 80s, and 90s, companies began to pop up that were more and more geared towards creating high quality gear to provide a safer ascent for these climbers. The early 2000s is where sport climbing and speed climbing began to pick up in popularity. Rock Climbers such as Chris Sharma, Tommy Caldwell, Alex Honnald, and others began popping out of the wood works. These modern-day climbers continue to help shape climbing as we know it.

1960s Timeline

  • 1961 : Royal Robbins, Chuck Pratt, and Tom Frost, lead Salathe Wall on El Capitan in 6-days (484 pitons, 13 bolts); 5.9 A4. Single push by Robbins & Frost in 1962.
  • 1961 : John Gill makes the first free ascent as an onsight, free solo, of the Thimble, 30 feet, in the Needles, South Dakota, USA, now considered the first-ever 7a+ (5.12a).
  • 1964 : Royal Robbins and Pat Ament free climb Athlete's Feat, Boulder CO. Five very short, hard pitches (5.11a. 5.10d, 5.10d, 5.10c, 5.9).[36] Most difficult multi-pitch in USA.
  • 1964 : Royal Robbins, Chuck Pratt, Tom Frost, and Yvon Chouinard climb the 28-pitch North American Wall on El Capitan, Yosemite, at 5.8/A5, was hardest aid climb.
  • 1965 : Yvon Chouinard, T M Herbert ascend, in a single push, the 32-pitch Muir Wall on El Cap, 5.9 A5. First new El Cap route by a party of two.
  • 1968 : Royal Robbins solos on aid (added aid bolt at crux) the Muir Wall on El Capitan, Yosemite, first time El Capitan is climbed alone (took nine days).
